Projects

  • CAISO Electricity Rate Design
    •

    Research Direction Proposal

    This current research direction of this project is to develop a dynamic electricity pricing model for CAISO using Avoided Cost time series data. By leveraging trends from existing pricing models and incorporating a penalty parameter for policy- and economics-driven regularization, the hybrid statistical model seeks to minimize deviation from market electricity values while promoting cost-efficiency, renewable energy adoption, and transparent pricing for consumers.

    Undergraduate Research Conference Presentation

    At UC San Diego’s URC, an inclusive event celebrating undergraduate research excellence, I presented my work modeling electricity pricing for SDG&E using CAISO data. I developed four benchmark statistical prediction models before conducting multivariable regression analyses for marginal and fixed costs, identifying the optimal combination of variables to accurately predict electricity rates. This project applies rigorous statistical modeling and data analysis to real-world energy systems.

  • Analyzing Forest Fire Severity in California
    •

    Files

    This project analyzed historical wildfire and weather data (2012–2015) in the United States to identify meteorological factors that predict wildfire severity, measured by acres burned. Using data integration and statistical analysis, I found that low humidity, lower temperatures, high wind speed, and high-pressure systems were associated with larger wildfires, while regions closer to the equator also tended to experience more severe fires. The study highlights key environmental drivers of wildfire risk and provides a framework for informing prevention strategies, resource allocation, and future predictive modeling.

  • Predicting Fat Content in Recipes - ML Project
    •

    Part 1

    In this project, I analyzed two datasets from Food.com: one containing nutritional and instructional information about recipes, and the other capturing user interactions and account data. I explored how recipe attributes, particularly nutritional content like fat levels, influence user ratings. The study focused on whether higher-fat recipes receive higher ratings on average, providing insights into how users balance taste and health. These findings can inform strategies to optimize recipe presentation and engagement on the platform.

    Part 2

    Building on my analysis from part 1, I developed a machine learning model to predict the total fat content of a recipe based on its features, including nutritional information and user ratings. Total fat was chosen as the response variable to further explore its relationship with other recipe attributes, building on prior findings that higher-fat recipes tend to receive higher ratings. I framed this as a regression problem and evaluated the model using both R² and RMSE scores to capture both correlation and large prediction errors. This project is an example of utilizing exploratory data insights into predictive modeling, and applying appropriate evaluation metrics for regression tasks.

Papers

  • Suicide Rates
    •

    This research applied statistical methods and data analysis to examine international suicide rates across different age groups. Using R, I conducted data cleaning, visualization, and statistical analysis with techniques such as ANOVA and Tukey's HSD tests, while addressing challenges like non-normal data distributions.

    The study investigated differences in suicide rates across age groups, testing the null hypothesis that rates do not differ versus the alternative that they do. A specific focus was on whether rates are highest among the 55–74 age group, informed by midlife crisis trends. Post-hoc comparisons were used to evaluate these hypotheses and identify significant patterns across age groups.
